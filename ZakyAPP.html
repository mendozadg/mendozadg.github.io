Zaky APP

a mobile app that encourages parents to provide skin-to-skin contact for their newborns and track feeding, diaper changes, and biometric information.
Client: Nurtured by Design

Summary

Role: UX Researcher, UX Designer
Tools Used: Sketch App, Mural.ly, InDesign, InVision, Lookback.io, Principle, Copic Markers
Class: Rapid Prototyping and Lean UX Methodology (Eric Nordquist, Clinical Associate Professor)
Date: January - May 2018

For my "Rapid Prototyping and Lean UX Methodology" class, we worked on individual projects for a client named Nurtured by Design, founded by Yamile Jackson. Yamile has been featured on Oprah for her products that help parents take care of newborns. For this particular project, I was tasked with developing a companion app for The Zaky ZAK, a garment that facilitates skin-to-skin contact.

For more information on Nurtured by Design's "The Zaky" brand and their products, visit their website.

This is an individual project, with each classmate making their own version of the product, providing Nurtured by Design with various prototype samples to guide their final design. However, we did create user scenarios and conduct "How Might We" exercises in small groups.

Process

Research Stage

Client Kickoff and Literature Review

The project began with a client kickoff meeting with Yamile Jackson, who gave our class a presentation on the importance of kangaroo care (a.k.a. skin-to-skin contact), her company's product offerings, and her newest endeavor - the creation of a medical-grade sensor that will be attached to The Zaky ZAK and will monitor the baby's biometrics. She then provided us with information on the accompanying app that she wanted our class to design, as well as a list of features, brand guidelines, and tone that we were to include in the app.

Competitive Evaluation

I then conducted a competitive evaluation of similar products on the market. Yamile Jackson had provided us with an idea of what she felt could be her product's value proposition: an in-app community of users, the ability to accurately track biometric data through manual entry and a medical-grade sensor on the kangaroo care garment, and in-app communication with caregivers and medical care providers.

Based on my evaluation, I recommended that for an early version of the product, attention be paid to the following features:

  • the ability to share information with doctors' offices and other caregivers;
  • tracking medications and symptoms (which had not been a requested feature);
  • in-app health records.

User Scenarios

Our class as a whole had a solid understanding of our target users for the product - parents who had recently had a baby, as well as any secondary caregivers that might help. For this reason, we did not feel the need to create personas. Instead, we split into groups to conduct "How Might We?" exercises that would inform our user scenarios, which we would use for our prototype and test script.

Scenario 1

Caregiver opens app and creates account, then logs diaper change.

Scenario 2

Caregiver breastfeeds baby while in kangaroo care and logs feeding in app.

Scenario 3

Caregiver views duration of kangaroo care session and continues until they reach the 1-hour mark.

Scenario 4

Caregiver receives alert about baby's high temperature, so they contact doctor about it.

Testing and Prototypes

User Testing with Low Fidelity Wireframes

To test my ideas, I used Facebook to recruit users who had had a child within the last two years. According to user research expert Jakob Nielsen, 85% of a system's problems can be discovered after testing with five users; the return on investment after five users is minimal. Therefore, I recruited five users (though one had a last-minute cancellation).

The prototype used in testing was a low-fidelity mockup of the app, to encourage participants to provide honest feedback despite their knowing that I was the designer behind the interface.

Key Findings from User Testing

My main takeaways from the four user test sessions were:

  • all users wanted the app to indicate what "normal" is, especially those who were new parents;
  • the app should be highly customizable - some parents wanted more push notifications, and others (especially those who are more experienced parents) felt that too many alerts were alarmist; and,
  • most users were skeptical about the community aspect of the app, and felt more comfortable relying on resources like WebMD rather than other users for advice about their baby's health.

Final Design Proposal

My final design of the app takes the findings from the user tests into consideration and places no emphasis on the community aspect that had been requested. Instead, I focused on the customization aspect, creating a dashboard of tiles similar to FitBit's mobile app that the user can move around based on what they find to be most important. There is also a gamification aspect to the app (again, similar to FitBit) in the form of a confetti celebration to encourage users to complete kangaroo sessions of a minimum duration of one-hour (as seen in the GIF below), which is the minimum amount of team that skin-to-skin contact must be provided to a baby for it to be beneficial. Lastly, I conducted a content strategy audit and tailored my messaging to be more information and less vague, to keep the guesswork out of the messaging and allow parents to fully understand the data about their child.
